Lean Principles

Learn how to maximize value by eliminating waste, optimizing flow, and continuously improving your processes

Lean Thinking and Practices

Lean thinking originated in manufacturing at Toyota and has since transformed industries worldwide. The core philosophy is simple: maximize value while minimizing waste.

Lean isn't just about cutting costs‚Äîit's about delivering more value to customers faster, with higher quality and less effort.

1 Define Value

Understand what customers truly value and are willing to pay for.

Example in Practice:

A software team interviewed 50 users and discovered they only used 3 of 15 features. By focusing on those 3, they reduced development time by 60% and increased customer satisfaction.

2 Map Value Stream

Identify all steps in your process and eliminate non-value-adding activities.

Example in Practice:

A development team mapped their deployment process and found 70% of time was spent waiting for approvals. They automated approvals for low-risk changes, cutting deployment time from 2 weeks to 2 days.

3 Create Flow

Make work flow smoothly without interruptions, delays, or bottlenecks.

Example in Practice:

A team eliminated handoffs by cross-training developers to handle design, code, and testing. This reduced their cycle time from 3 weeks to 5 days and eliminated context-switching waste.

4 Establish Pull

Only produce what customers need, when they need it‚Äîavoid overproduction.

Example in Practice:

Instead of building a year's worth of features upfront, a startup released a basic MVP in 2 weeks, got customer feedback, then built only the features users requested. 40% of original planned features were never needed.

5 Seek Perfection

Continuously improve through small, incremental changes (Kaizen).

Example in Practice:

A team holds 15-minute retrospectives every Friday. Each week they implement one small improvement. After 6 months, they'd made 24 improvements that collectively reduced bugs by 45%.

6 Respect People

Empower teams to identify problems and implement solutions.

Example in Practice:

A manager gave teams authority to stop production if they spotted quality issues. Defects dropped 80% because workers felt empowered to fix problems immediately rather than passing them along.
