Lean thinking started in 1913 with Henry Ford. At his Highland Park, Michigan plant, Ford created what he called "flow production." He lined up all the fabrication steps in order and got parts flowing through fast. His system was revolutionary, but it had a problem. It wasn't varied. Every Model T was basically the same. Around 1918, Sakichi Toyoda started his business in Japan with a patent for an automatic loom. By 1950, his successors Eiji Toyoda and Taiichi Ohno went to America to study manufacturing. They looked at Ford's system and saw the flaws. Companies were making huge batches of parts that just sat in inventory, and they cared more about production volume than what customers actually wanted. So Toyota took Ford's flow ideas and figured out how to add flexibility. That became the Toyota Production System. The word "lean" didn't come along until 1990 when it was coined in the book "The Machine That Changed the World."

1913
Henry Ford creates flow production at Highland Park, Michigan. He lines up fabrication steps in order to keep things moving fast.
1918 to 1935
Sakichi Toyoda starts his business with an automatic loom patent. The company later switches to making cars and trucks.
1950
Eiji Toyoda and Taiichi Ohno travel to the US to study American manufacturing. They spot major flaws in Western production methods.
1965
Toyota rolls out its Production System to suppliers and starts documenting how it works.
1973
The oil crisis hits. Everyone suddenly notices how much better Toyota's methods work compared to other car makers.
1990
"The Machine That Changed the World" gets published. This is where the term "lean" comes from. The book shows just how far ahead Toyota is.
2000s to Now
Lean spreads way beyond manufacturing. Now you see it in software development, healthcare, retail, and pretty much every industry.

One really cool application of Lean showed up in software development through Kanban. It started as a scheduling system with visual cards on the factory floor at Toyota. In 2004, David J. Anderson figured out how to adapt it for software teams. Kanban helps teams see their workflow, limit how much work they're doing at once, and keep improving. It basically brought Lean principles straight into modern software development.
