Lean principles have roots around 1918 when Sakichi Toyoda established his business with a patent for an automatic loom. After selling the patents in 1929, the company reinvented itself in the automotive industry. By 1950, Japanese auto production was minimal compared to the US. Eiji Toyoda was sent to study American manufacturing methods, joined by Taiichi Ohno, who reasoned that Western production systems had major flaws: producing components in large batches resulted in large inventories, and methods preferred large production over customer preferences. Through iteration, the Toyota Production System evolved, using innovation and common knowledge in an environment with different cultural values. The system was largely unnoticed until the 1973 oil crisis, and the term "lean" was coined in 1990 in the book "The Machine That Changed the World."

1918-1935
Sakichi Toyoda establishes business with automatic loom patent; company transitions to automotive industry; truck and car production begins
1950
Eiji Toyoda and Taiichi Ohno study US manufacturing methods and identify flaws in Western production systems
1965
Toyota Production System rolled out to suppliers and begins to be documented
1973
Oil crisis brings global attention to Toyota's superior production methods
1990
"The Machine That Changed the World" published; term "lean" coined; performance gaps between Toyota and other automakers highlighted
2000s-Present
Lean principles expand beyond manufacturing into software development, healthcare, services, and virtually every industry sector

One particularly influential application of Lean thinking emerged in software development through Kanban. Originally a scheduling system using visual cards in Toyota's factories, Kanban was adapted for software teams in 2004 by David J. Anderson. This approach helps teams visualize workflow, limit work in progress, and continuously improve—bringing Lean principles directly into modern software development practices.
